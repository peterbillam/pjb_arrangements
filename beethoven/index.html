Editor's notes

  • Only the first half of Mies' book is present here. The rest, also important, discusses:
    III. Unity within the movement and in the complete works, and groups of works,
    IV. Constant features in the development: (in motive and rhythm, in modulation, in instrumentation, and in the general plan),
    V. Beethoven as creative artist: (Writing the sketches, Artistic creation, Aesthetic judgement, and the importance of the sketches to Beethoven)
    VI. Beethoven as composer of instrumental music and as writer of songs
    VII. Style features adopted for their expressive value: (The common chord and other structures in the melody, Suspension and descending seconds, Ornaments, the Character of the different keys, and Notation and musical expression)
  • Beethoven sketched in the single line. A historical progression is clear here, corresponding to the stylistic evolution; Brahms sketched in two lines, tune+bass, and Mahler sketched in open-score quartet.
  • Beethoven was taught by Neefe to always carry a sketchbook, and to sketch moods of the moment (eg: laughter, flirtatiousness, anger, resignation, determination, fickleness, contentment, love, friendship etc., whether by introspection or observation), and facts of Nature (eg: earth, water, air, fire, dawn, lightening, forest etc.). He also taught particular moods as associated with particular tonalities, and the meaningfulness of Numbers within nature and within music, and encouraged Beethoven to play Bach's Well-Tempered Clavier.
  • Beethoven often sketched without keysigs or even clefs; I've added them here.
  • The musical examples were typeset, and the midi-files generated, by muscript. All their source-files are available in a tarball at www.pjb.com.au/mus/beethoven/motifs.tgz and on github at github.com/peterbillam/pjb_arrangements

Peter Billam
